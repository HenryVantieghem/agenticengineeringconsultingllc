This is a design problem, not a budget problem. Corporate security was built for offices. The office moved home three years ago. The security model didn't follow.

What's your weakest link? I'd bet it's not your office network.

## Post 4: 5 Things Every Family Office Should Do This Week (How-To / Value-First)

Your family office has a financial advisor, an estate lawyer, and a tax strategist.

But I'd bet there's no one responsible for your family's personal cybersecurity.

Here are 5 things you can do this week. All free. All take less than an hour total.

1. Run a home WiFi scan on every family member's network. Most routers expose connected devices, open ports, and firmware vulnerabilities. It takes 3 minutes per household.

2. Check if any family email addresses appear in data breach databases. HaveIBeenPwned is free and instant. You might be surprised by what's already out there.

3. Enable hardware security keys for all financial accounts. SMS two-factor authentication can be bypassed with a $50 SIM swap. Physical keys cannot.

4. Brief family members on deepfake voice calls. AI can clone a voice from 3 seconds of audio. Yes, this applies to teenagers too. Especially teenagers.

5. Get personal cyber insurance separate from your corporate policy. Most corporate policies explicitly exclude personal devices and home network incidents. A personal policy closes that gap.

Which of these 5 has your family actually done? Be honest.

#CyberTips #FamilyOffice #WealthManagement #DigitalSafety #Privacy

---

**Why this works:** Value-first educational posts build trust faster than any other format. The opening line uses a familiar pattern (advisor, lawyer, tax strategist) and reveals the missing piece, which creates an "aha" moment. Numbered lists perform well on LinkedIn because they're scannable and promise a clear payoff. Each step is genuinely actionable with no product pitch embedded. The "especially teenagers" aside adds personality and relatability. The closing question ("Be honest") creates a psychological loop. Readers audit themselves, feel a gap, and comment to process it publicly. Posts that make people self-assess drive 3x more comments than posts that simply inform.

The families most at risk are the ones most visible. Public speakers. Executives on earnings calls. Philanthropists at galas. Anyone whose voice exists on the internet.

Most families have no verification protocol for large financial requests. No code word. No callback procedure. No secondary confirmation channel.

The fraud works because it relies on trust. And trust is the one thing security software can't patch.

Honest question: does your family have a verification protocol for large financial requests? Drop your answer below.
